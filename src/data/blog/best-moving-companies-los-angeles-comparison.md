## What Makes a Moving Company Legitimate in LA

Before comparing companies, understand the minimum requirements for legal operation:

**California PUC License:** Required for all moves within California. Verify at cpuc.ca.gov. Unlicensed movers can't legally operate and have no oversight.

**USDOT Number:** Required for interstate moves. Check at safer.fmcsa.dot.gov. Without this, companies can't legally cross state lines.

**Insurance Coverage:** Legitimate movers carry cargo insurance (protects your belongings) and liability insurance (protects property damage). Always request proof.

**Workers' Compensation:** Protects you from lawsuits if movers get injured. California requires this for all employees.

**Physical Address:** Real companies have actual locations, not just PO boxes. Drive by if suspicious—many "companies" are just brokers.

## Red Flags to Avoid

Watch for these warning signs when researching companies:

**Price Too Good to Be True:** $50/hour usually means unlicensed, uninsured, or bait-and-switch tactics. Professional movers can't operate profitably below $80/hour.

**Cash Only Operations:** Legitimate companies accept cards, checks, and provide receipts. Cash-only means no accountability.

**No Physical Address:** Google Street View their location. Many "companies" are just apartment-based brokers.

**Deposit Over 20%:** California law limits deposits. Large upfront payments risk disappearing companies.

**Blank or Vague Contracts:** Everything should be detailed in writing—rates, dates, inventory, insurance coverage.

Before signing examine:

- Cancellation policies- Damage claim procedures- Delivery windows (for long-distance)- Payment terms and methods
